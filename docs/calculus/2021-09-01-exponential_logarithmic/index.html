    • ap+q=ap⋅aqa^{p+q} = a^p \cdot a^qap+q=ap⋅aq

    Euler’s number eee

    By concept, the number eee or Euler’s number, is a mathematical constant which is an irrational number of

    e=2.71828182845904523536028747135266249775724709369995… e = 2.71828182845904523536028747135266249775724709369995\dots e=2.71828182845904523536028747135266249775724709369995…

    There are multiple approaches existed to explain the origin of this “unique” constant (just like how π\piπ is the ratio between circumference and diameter of any circle). However, this note will limit the scope to two explanations (or characterization) of this special constant:

    • eee as a base rate of exponential growth (Azad, 2021)
    • The higher boundary for Area under the curve of 1x\frac{1}{x}x1​ to be equal 1.

    Area under the curve of y=1xy = \frac{1}{x}y=x1​

    Figure 1: Area under the curve of 1x\frac{1}{x}x1​ (Singh, 2021)

    The second popular characterization of eee is using Geometry, to be specific, the area under the curve of y=1/xy = 1/xy=1/x

    To approximate the area under the curve of y=1/xy=1/xy=1/x in which x>0x > 0x>0, let seperate the area into adjacent rectangles with the base of Δx=1\Delta{x} = 1Δx=1 and height of 1/x01/{x_0}1/x0​. In other words, he are under the curve, which we will called A\mathbf{A}A, consists of infinite smaller rectangles with base 111:

    A=∑i=1∞A(i,i+1)≈∑i=0∞(Δxi)≈∑i=0∞(1i) \begin{aligned} \mathbf{A} &= \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}{\mathbf{A}(i, i+1)}\\ &\approx \sum_{i=0}^{\infty}{\left(\frac{\Delta{x}}{i}\right)}\\ &\approx \sum_{i=0}^{\infty}{\left(\frac{1}{i}\right)} \end{aligned} A​=i=1∑∞​A(i,i+1)≈i=0∑∞​(iΔx​)≈i=0∑∞​(i1​)​

    The smaller the base is, the more accurate the area sum becomes:

    A(a,b)=lim⁡Δx→0[∑i=0a+iΔx≤b(Δxa+iΔx)] \mathbf{A}(a,b) = \lim_{\Delta{x}\to 0}{\left[\sum_{i=0}^{a+i\Delta{x} \leq b}{\left(\frac{\Delta{x}}{a+i\Delta{x}}\right)}\right]} A(a,b)=Δx→0lim​[i=0∑a+iΔx≤b​(a+iΔxΔx​)]

    Suppose we have a transformation ϕ:(x,y)↦(2x,y/2)\phi : (x, y) \mapsto (2x, y/2)ϕ:(x,y)↦(2x,y/2). As (2x0,1/2x0)(2x_0, 1/{2x_0})(2x0​,1/2x0​) is also located on the curve of y=1/xy=1/xy=1/x and the area of every member rectangle before the transformation remains the same after the transformation (base doubled, height halved), we clearly see that:

    A(a,b)=A(2a,2b)(Figure 1 - left)=A(ka,kb)k∈R \begin{aligned} \mathbf{A}(a,b) &= \mathbf{A}(2a,2b) & \text{(Figure 1 - left)}\\ &= \mathbf{A}(ka, kb) & k\isin\mathbb{R} \end{aligned} A(a,b)​=A(2a,2b)=A(ka,kb)​(Figure 1 - left)k∈R​

    In other words

    A(1,ab)=A(1,a)+A(a,ab)=A(1,a)+A(1,b) \begin{aligned} \mathbf{A}(1,ab) &= \mathbf{A}(1, a) + \mathbf{A}{(a,ab)}\\ &= \mathbf{A}(1,a) + \mathbf{A}(1,b) \end{aligned} A(1,ab)​=A(1,a)+A(a,ab)=A(1,a)+A(1,b)​

    … which is similar to a property of logarithm functions:

    ln⁡ab=ln⁡a+ln⁡b \ln{ab} = \ln{a} + \ln{b} lnab=lna+lnb
